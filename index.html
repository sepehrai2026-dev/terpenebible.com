Introduction

Terpenes are aromatic compounds found in virtually every plant. They give lavender its calming scent, citrus its zing, and pine forests their freshness. Beyond aroma, terpenes have documented biological effects — anti-inflammatory, anxiolytic, analgesic, and more. Whether you're into aromatherapy, cannabis, cooking, or botanical science, understanding terpenes transforms how you experience the plant world.

Terpene profiles

Myrcene

Earthy

The most abundant terpene in cannabis and hops, myrcene delivers an earthy, musky aroma with hints of ripe fruit. It's associated with relaxing, sedative effects and is believed to enhance the absorption of other compounds.

    Aroma: earthy, musky, herbal, fruity,Found in: mangoes, hops, lemongrass, thyme,Research: anti-inflammatory, analgesic properties,Boiling point: 167°C (332°F),Most common terpene in modern cannabis
Learn about myrcene →

Limonene

Citrus

The quintessential citrus terpene, limonene gives oranges, lemons, and grapefruits their bright, uplifting aroma. It's one of the most well-studied terpenes, with research suggesting mood-elevating and stress-reducing properties.

    Aroma: bright citrus, orange, lemon,Found in: citrus rinds, juniper, rosemary,Research: anxiolytic, anti-depressant effects,Boiling point: 176°C (349°F),High bioavailability when inhaled
Learn about limonene →

Linalool

Floral

The signature terpene of lavender, linalool has a sweet, floral aroma that has been used for centuries in traditional relaxation and sleep remedies. It's one of the most extensively researched terpenes for calming effects.

    Aroma: floral, sweet, lavender, spicy,Found in: lavender, coriander, mint, cinnamon,Research: anxiolytic, sedative, anti-convulsant,Boiling point: 198°C (388°F),Used extensively in perfumery
Learn about linalool →

Pinene

Fresh

The most widely occurring terpene in nature, pinene is what makes pine forests smell so refreshing. It exists in two forms — alpha-pinene (pine scent) and beta-pinene (herbal, dill-like). Research suggests it may support respiratory function and alertness.

    Aroma: sharp pine, fresh, woody,Found in: pine needles, rosemary, basil, dill,Research: bronchodilator, anti-inflammatory,Boiling point: 155°C (311°F),Most abundant terpene in the natural world
Learn about pinene →

Caryophyllene

Spicy

Unique among terpenes, beta-caryophyllene can bind to CB2 cannabinoid receptors, making it the only terpene that acts as a dietary cannabinoid. It delivers a warm, spicy, peppery aroma and is abundant in black pepper, cloves, and cinnamon.

    Aroma: spicy, peppery, woody, warm,Found in: black pepper, cloves, cinnamon, oregano,Research: CB2 receptor agonist, anti-inflammatory,Boiling point: 130°C (266°F),Only terpene that binds cannabinoid receptors
Learn about caryophyllene →

Terpinolene

Complex

The least common of the major terpenes, terpinolene has a complex, multi-layered aroma that combines floral, herbal, piney, and slightly citrusy notes. It's found in lilacs, tea tree, and nutmeg, and is associated with uplifting, energetic effects.

    Aroma: floral, herbal, piney, citrusy,Found in: lilac, tea tree, nutmeg, apples,Research: antioxidant, mildly sedative at high doses,Boiling point: 186°C (366°F),Rarest of the primary terpenes

03

Understanding terpenes

What are terpenes?

Terpenes are organic hydrocarbons produced by plants as part of their essential oils. They evolved as defense mechanisms against herbivores and to attract pollinators. Over 20,000 terpenes have been identified, but a handful dominate most plants we interact with daily.

The entourage effect

Terpenes don't work in isolation. Research suggests they interact synergistically with other plant compounds, modulating and enhancing effects. This 'entourage effect' is why whole-plant extracts often feel different from isolated compounds.

How to identify terpenes

Your nose is your best tool. Each terpene has a distinct aroma profile — earthy, citrusy, floral, piney, spicy, or herbal. With practice, you can identify dominant terpenes in essential oils, herbs, and other botanical products by scent alone.

Terpenes in everyday life

You encounter terpenes constantly — in the zest of a lemon (limonene), the scent of a pine tree (pinene), the earthiness of fresh hops (myrcene), and the spice of black pepper (caryophyllene). Understanding them enriches cooking, gardening, and wellness.
